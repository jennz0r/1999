First Things First

Research!

My first email redesign victim was our Welcome email, so I started researching other companies' Welcome emails.

Signed up for services and scoured my personal email for examples of good and/or bad email designs.

A lot of it is pretty instinctual, but eventually I could pinpoint the highs and lows.

Least Favorites

  • Lumi
  • HackerLeague
  • Super text heavy. I'm not going to read all that!
  • Lumi's calls to action are not noticeable and the email doesn't show how to unsubscribe.
  • HackerLeague's calls to action are repeated and don't use images where they make sense (Twitter).

Favorites

  • DSW
  • Creative Market
  • Creative Market
  • DSW uses just one image and font types for design features. Their calls to action are plentiful, but obvious, with the most important ones shown with highlights!
  • Creative Market makes sense because they're a designer focused website.
  • Their calls to action are obvious - Edit Profile or Open a Shop and they have beautiful image content to add a layer of interest.

Most Favoritest

  • Radiolab
  • indiegogo


I know, I know, these are not welcome emails.

But they're just so pretty!

  • Radiolab does a great job with color interest as well as making a few key calls to action super obvious.
  • Indiegogo is great at using awesome images and making their calls to action brightly colored, consistent, and obvious.

Takeaways

  • Text light
  • Image heavy
  • Huge top anchored logo
  • Max width
  • Use a logo similar to website
  • Ensure you don't have to read across entire page

Responsiveness

Litmus Article

Scalable

Testing the mobile waters

Reliable rendering across clients and devices

Teams with limited resources

  • A simple (usually single-column) layout that scales down nicely.
  • Large text that is readable on screens of all sizes.
  • Large, touch-friendly calls-to-action.

Fluid or Liquid

Simple layouts

Emails with mostly text that can flow

Teams with limited resources

  • Percentage-based sizing so tables and images adapt to the screen size
  • Similar to scalable ones - don’t purposefully alter the layout or content of an email, but “flow” to fill space on the screen.
  • Typically work best for text-heavy layouts since there’s less control over how copy and images interact.

Responsive

Large mobile audiences

The most control over layout

Teams with knowledge of media queries

  • Everything from scalable and fluid emails with more control via CSS media queries.
  • Unlike Fluid, uses media queries to change layout of emails, sizes of text, images, and buttons, and hide or even swap content between desktop and mobile devices.

Things I Learned After Release

Oops

<Img> Within <a>

Super weird and only occurs occasionally.

VS.

High Res Images for Retina

The Problem

Outlook 2007, 2010, 2013, and Outlook in IE don't allow HTML or CSS image sizing. Argh.


The Solution

Replace <img> by setting a background-img style on the surrounding <td> and assigning display: none; on the <img>.

Campaign Monitor


@media all and (min-device-pixel-ratio : 1.5), 
    all and (-webkit-min-device-pixel-ratio: 1.5) {

    td[class="cautionBar"] {
        background-image: url('http://filenet.hotpads.com/images/email/warning.png');
        background-position: center;
        background-size: 30px 30px;
        background-repeat: no-repeat;
        width: 30px; !important;
        height: 30px; !important;
    }

    td[class="cautionBar"] img {
        display: none;
    }

}
